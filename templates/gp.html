General Period

During a person’s menstrual cycle they may have a lot of queries about their body and what may be happening. Variations in the colour and smell of one’s blood, the heaviness of one’s flow, and the symptoms one may experience are all common.

Period Symptoms

The release of these hormones, throughout the cycle may cause PMS, known as premenstrual syndrome. This is a condition which affects the body days before the actual period begins. It usually has the same symptoms of periods. These may include, but are not limited to:

  • Acne
  • Anxiety
  • Bloating
  • Body pain or soreness (commonly in the abdomen, breasts and lower back)
  • Changes in Appetite or Loss of Appetite
  • Cramps
  • Cravings
  • Constipation
  • Diarrhea
  • Dizziness
  • Fatigue or general tiredness
  • Headaches
  • Heavy Flow
  • Insomnia
  • Light Flow
  • Lymph Nodes (commonly near the breasts or armpit)
  • Mood Swings
  • Nausea
  • Spotting
  • Weight Gain or Loss.

Cramps are one of the most common symptoms. These can be sharp pains felt in the lower stomach or back. These pains can range from irritable or extremely painful. Some remedies for cramps are a recommended dose of painkillers (like ibuprofen or panadol), a warm compress or heating pad, taking a warm bath, drinking warm ginger tea, exercise or stretching.

Irregular Periods

Sometimes, one may have irregular periods. This is very common for the first few years of one’s period (for teenagers). This can be seen by fluctuations in the lengths of your period or menstrual cycle. Lateness in periods is also common. A late period is when your period has not started 5 or more days after the expected start date. However, this may also be a sign of pregnancy or another medical illness. There are other scenarios where you may completely miss your period. This is when you do not experience a period, or menstrual flow, for 6 or more weeks after your last period. This can also be normal.

If you are just starting your cycle or period is irregular, there is probably no need to worry. For teenagers, periods are usually irregular as they begin to regulate as their bodies mature.

Discharge

When your period is coming, you may notice your body producing white, slimy, odorless discharge. This is normal to the menstrual cycle. As you near the end of your period, you may begin to notice your body producing black-ish red, or dark brown discharge. This is normal as well. This discharge consists of old uterus lining which has been shed.

Cycle Lengths

One’s menstrual cycle is the (usually) monthly period or sequence in which a woman’s body prepares for the chance of pregnancy. This includes:

  • The period : where the uterus lining is being shed and comes out of the vagina as what seems to be blood. The period lasts about 3 to 7 days usually. The follicular phase: where the body produces a lot of hormones in order for the uterus to regrow a thick lining.
  • The ovulation phase: where an egg is released from the ovaries.
  • And the luteal phase: where the egg passes through the fallopian tube to the uterus where it will wait to possibly be fertilized. In this time period, many hormones are being released to prepare the body for pregnancy.

It should be noted that the average cycle length is 28 days, however any cycle between 21 to 40 days is normal. You may track your period with a calendar, or a free application (app) you may download on your smartphone. This allows you to know when your period may be coming and to prepare.

Seek Medical Help

If your cramps, or any other symptoms, become severe, you may need to see a doctor. This may include:

  • Frequent Heavy bleeding
  • Extremely painful cramps
  • Long Lasting cramps (for the entirety of your period or more)
  • Bleeding between periods
  • Fainting
  • Greenish or Yellowish discharge
  • Any other symptom deemed as an emergency

Or if you expect you may have any of the following:

  • Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)
  • Endometriosis
  • Amenorrhea
  • Ovarian Cysts
